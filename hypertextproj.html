The Hypertext Project was an assignment on making something akin to hypertext fiction, using Walt Whitman's Song of Myself as our starting text. Then, we were to make "enhancements" to it, which would include links to other sections of the poem and other sites as we saw fit.

It's not as heavily interconnected/linked up as the hypertext fiction we explored in class. Still, I think it turned out pretty enough.

Process Statement:

My primary ethos behind the changes I made in formatting was to reformat the text in a way that I thought it would originally be read. Old-timey literature like this sounds very flat and nonsensical to the modern mind since our vocabulary has changed so much from the day it was written - we can grammatically understand the sentence, maybe, but we miss a lot of cultural context. When you hear it read aloud, though, when you hear an actual human voice put emotion into it? It's not a substitute for a modern translation, but it helps comprehension.

The biggest changes that followed this were playing around with line breaks and indentation. The "staircase" of words, as Hena put it, was meant to evoke the tone of voice of going on and on with listing out terms in a list - a list that long looks messy in text, but maybe sounds better out loud, and looks better when each element has some space around it.

There was a part in section 4 where Whitman lists out many "sicknesses," beginning with personal afflictions and then moving on to matters of national importance. There are sections where Whitman talks about himself in a positive way. I tended to interpolate emotions here and format them to communicate in a way I thought would send a message. I decreased the font size of the personal afflictions and put them in a block, then increased the font size of the larger things, as if embarrassed to compare two categories of such different magnitudes. I tended to put some words in those positive sentences centered in their own lines, as if it was said with firm satisfaction. Maybe it's not how Whitman would've wanted it to be read, but unless you know how it should be read, I think it's better than a flat block of text.

I played around with the font, color, and size of certain words to make them pop and break up the monotony of the text wall further. I did this with words where the meaning might be more strongly associated with the format: color words were colored, visual descriptors had fonts that somewhat matched them, negation words were mirrored.

I didn't take much inspiration from anywhere in particular. I added in some images where I thought they might fit the most naturally per the suggestion of my peer review group. I wanted to keep my formatting relatively simple to evoke the same feeling as the very old hypertext literature - like Shelley Jackson's "my body".
